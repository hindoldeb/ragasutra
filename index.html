The Artist

Hindol Deb

Master Sitarist of the Maihar & Rampur Gharanas

A Tale of Two Traditions

Hindol Deb is an internationally renowned Indian classical sitarist whose artistry is defined by a profound adherence to his traditional heritage and a visionary quest for musical expansion. Representing the celebrated Maihar and Rampur Gharanas, Hindol has established himself as a virtuoso who bridges the ancient spiritual depth of the Raga with the infinite possibilities of global contemporary music.

Hindol's journey began at age five under his father, Sri Panchanan Sardar. He underwent rigorous training with Pandit Santosh Banerjee (Rampur Gharana) and Pandit Deepak Chaudhury (Maihar Gharana), a disciple of Pandit Ravi Shankar. Deeply influenced by masters like Ali Akbar Khan, Nikhil Banerji, and Zia-Mohiuddin Dagar, Hindol developed a unique style by integrating the meditative Dhrupad Ang and the vocal-like Been Ang (Veena style) onto the sitar.

His performances are celebrated for their technical finesse and emotional maturity, leading critic Prakash Wadhera to remark that his playing is marked by 'profound maturity.' To date, he has released four albums dedicated to the purity of Indian classical music.

"Music is the space between the notes — and the raga lives in that space. My journey is about honoring that sacred space while expanding its boundaries."

— Hindol Deb

The Project

Ragasutra

Pure Indian Classical Music in the Traditional Raga Format

Ragasutra — meaning "the thread of Raga" — is Hindol Deb's most personal and profound musical offering. It is a return to the source, a dedication to the purity of the Hindustani classical tradition in its most essential form: a solo sitar accompanied by the tabla, unfolding in the ancient format of the raga.

In Ragasutra, there are no concessions to modernity, no fusion, no compromise. It is Indian classical music as it has been practiced for centuries — a meditative journey through the architecture of a raga, from the slow, introspective Alap to the rhythmically exhilarating Gat and Jhala. Each performance is a unique and unrepeatable event, a conversation between the sitarist, the tabla player, and the raga itself.

The name Ragasutra evokes the ancient Sanskrit concept of the sutra — a thread or aphorism that contains within it a vast universe of meaning. Just as a sutra is a condensed vessel of wisdom, the raga is a condensed vessel of emotion and spirit. Hindol's Ragasutra performances seek to unravel that thread, revealing the infinite depth and beauty contained within each raga.

"Indian classical music is contemplative and ecstatic at the same time. Come and experience the magic unfold."

— Hindol Deb on Ragasutra

The Ragas

Ragas of the Repertoire

Each raga is a universe — a specific emotional and temporal landscape

Bhimpalasi
Afternoon · Karuna & Bhakti
A raga of deep longing and devotion, evoking the tender emotions of the afternoon hours. Its pentatonic ascending scale gives it a haunting, meditative quality.
Yaman
Evening · Shringara & Bhakti
One of the most beloved ragas of the evening, Yaman is associated with romance and devotion. Its raised fourth (tivra Ma) gives it an ethereal, expansive quality.
Hemant
Late Evening · Shanta & Karuna
A raga of the early winter season, Hemant evokes a sense of quiet contemplation and gentle melancholy. Its pentatonic structure creates a serene and introspective atmosphere.
Gawoti
Late Night · Vira & Adbhuta
A rare and majestic raga of the late night, Gawoti is known for its grandeur and power. Its unique combination of notes creates a sense of awe and wonder.
Kirwani
Night · Karuna & Shringara
A raga of profound emotional depth, Kirwani is known for its ability to evoke both joy and sorrow simultaneously. Its distinctive notes create a bittersweet, poignant atmosphere.
Desh
Late Evening · Shringara & Bhakti
A raga associated with the monsoon season and romantic longing, Desh is one of the most popular and beloved ragas in the Hindustani tradition. Its flowing phrases evoke the beauty of nature.

The Tradition

Indian Classical Music

One of the world's oldest and most sophisticated musical traditions

Indian classical music is one of the oldest and most sophisticated musical traditions in the world, with roots stretching back over three thousand years to the ancient Sama Veda, the sacred hymns of the Vedic civilization. It is a living tradition, continuously evolving yet deeply rooted in its ancient origins.

The tradition is divided into two main streams: Hindustani (North Indian) and Carnatic (South Indian). Hindol Deb's Ragasutra is firmly rooted in the Hindustani tradition, which was profoundly shaped by the Mughal court culture of the 16th and 17th centuries, when Persian and Central Asian musical influences merged with the ancient Indian tradition to create a uniquely rich and complex art form.

At the heart of Indian classical music are two fundamental concepts: the Raga (melodic framework) and the Tala (rhythmic cycle). These two elements, in constant dialogue and interplay, form the basis of all Indian classical music performance.

✦ ✦ ✦
🎵

The Raga

A raga is not merely a scale. It is a living entity with its own personality, mood, time of day, and season. Each raga prescribes not only which notes to use, but how to approach them, which to emphasize, and which ornaments to employ. A skilled musician uses the raga as a framework for spontaneous improvisation, creating a unique performance each time.

🥁

The Tala

The tala is the rhythmic cycle that underlies all Indian classical music. Unlike Western time signatures, the tala is a cyclical structure of beats that can range from simple cycles of 4 or 8 beats to complex cycles of 7, 10, 12, or 16 beats. The interplay between the melodic improvisation of the soloist and the rhythmic dialogue of the tabla creates the dynamic tension at the heart of Indian classical music.

🌅

Time & Season

One of the most remarkable aspects of Indian classical music is its deep connection to time and nature. Each raga is associated with a specific time of day or season. Morning ragas evoke the freshness of dawn; evening ragas capture the melancholy of dusk; monsoon ragas conjure the drama of the rains. This temporal dimension gives Indian classical music a profound sense of context and meaning.

🧘

Improvisation

Indian classical music is fundamentally an improvisational art. While the raga provides a framework of rules and notes, the musician is free to explore its possibilities in real time, creating a unique performance each time. This improvisation is not random, however — it is guided by a deep understanding of the raga's character and the rules of the tradition. The result is a music that is simultaneously structured and free.

🎓

Guru-Shishya Parampara

The transmission of Indian classical music has traditionally taken place through the guru-shishya parampara — the teacher-student tradition. In this system, the student lives with the teacher for many years, absorbing not just the technical knowledge of the music but its spirit, philosophy, and way of life. This intimate form of transmission ensures that the subtle nuances and oral traditions of the music are preserved across generations.

🏛️

The Gharana System

A gharana is a school or lineage of Indian classical music, each with its own distinctive style, techniques, and repertoire. The major gharanas of sitar music include the Maihar Gharana, associated with Ravi Shankar, and the Rampur Gharana. Hindol Deb represents both of these great traditions, bringing their combined wisdom and artistry to his Ragasutra performances.

The Technique

The Art of Microtonal Bends

The Soul of the Sitar — Meend, Gamak & the Infinite Space Between Notes

What makes the sitar one of the most expressive instruments in the world is its extraordinary ability to bend and slide between notes — to inhabit the infinite microtonal space that lies between the fixed pitches of Western music. This is the realm of meend, gamak, andolan, and murki — the ornaments that give Indian classical music its distinctive voice and emotional depth.

In Western music, notes are fixed points — discrete steps on a staircase. In Indian classical music, notes are more like regions of a landscape, with infinite gradations of pitch between them. The musician does not merely play a note; they approach it, inhabit it, and depart from it in ways that carry profound emotional meaning.

On the sitar, these microtonal inflections are achieved by pulling the string sideways across the fret, stretching it to raise the pitch. A skilled sitarist can bend a note by a quarter tone, a half tone, or even a full tone, creating a seamless glide that mimics the nuances of the human voice. This technique, known as meend, is the most fundamental ornament of Hindustani classical music.

"The beauty of the sitar lies in its ability to bend and slide between notes in ways that Western instruments cannot. These ornaments are not mere decorations — they are the very soul of the raga, breathing life and emotion into the music."

— Hindol Deb, The Raga Notebook

The microtonal inflections of Indian classical music are not arbitrary. Each raga prescribes specific ornaments for specific notes, and a musician who uses the wrong ornament on the wrong note will immediately reveal their lack of understanding of the raga's character. The ornaments are, in a sense, the grammar of the raga — the rules that govern how the notes are to be spoken.

The Essential Ornaments

  • Meend A smooth, continuous glide from one note to another, achieved by pulling the string sideways across the fret. Meend creates a sense of fluidity, grace, and vocal quality. It is the most fundamental ornament of Hindustani classical music, and the one that most clearly distinguishes it from Western music.
  • Gamak A rapid, forceful oscillation between two notes, creating a powerful and dramatic effect. Gamak is used to add energy, intensity, and rhythmic drive to a performance. It is particularly associated with the Dhrupad style of singing and the Been Ang style of sitar playing.
  • Andolan A gentle, slow oscillation around a note, creating a sense of subtle vibrato. Andolan is used to add warmth and expressiveness to specific notes in a raga, particularly those that are considered to have a special emotional significance.
  • Murki A rapid, ornamental turn around a note, similar to a trill or mordent in Western music. Murki adds grace and ornamentation to a phrase, creating a sense of lightness and agility.
  • Kan A grace note — a brief touch of an adjacent note before landing on the main note. Kan adds a sense of approach and direction to a note, giving it a sense of arrival and emphasis.
  • Krintan A hammer-on or pull-off technique, where a note is produced by the left hand alone without plucking. Krintan creates a smooth, connected quality between notes, adding to the vocal character of the sitar.

The Instrument

The Sitar

A resonant universe of strings, sympathetics, and infinite expression

  • Origin
    The sitar evolved in the Indian subcontinent during the Mughal era (16th–17th centuries), combining elements of the ancient veena with the Persian setar. Its name derives from the Persian se tar — "three strings."
  • Main Strings
    6–7 main playing strings, tuned to the notes of the raga. The sitarist plucks these strings with a wire plectrum (mizrab) worn on the index finger of the right hand.
  • Sympathetic Strings
    11–13 sympathetic strings (tarab) run beneath the main strings, tuned to the notes of the raga. These strings vibrate in sympathy with the main strings, creating the characteristic shimmering resonance of the sitar.
  • Curved Frets
    The sitar's curved, movable frets allow the player to bend notes by pulling the string sideways, enabling the microtonal inflections that are central to Indian classical music.
  • Resonator
    The main resonator is a large gourd (tumba) at the base of the instrument, with a smaller gourd sometimes attached at the top. The gourd amplifies the sound and gives the sitar its distinctive warm, resonant tone.
  • Length
    Approximately 1.2 meters (4 feet) in length, the sitar is played seated on the floor, with the instrument resting diagonally across the player's body.

The Rhythmic Heart

The Tabla Players

The masters of rhythm who have brought Ragasutra to life

In the tradition of Indian classical music, the tabla player is not merely an accompanist — they are a co-creator, an equal partner in the musical dialogue. The tabla player must listen deeply to the soloist, responding to their every nuance and inflection, while simultaneously maintaining the rhythmic cycle and exploring its own creative possibilities. Hindol Deb has been fortunate to collaborate with some of the finest tabla players of his generation.

Shankh Lahiri
Tabla Artist · Tampa, Florida, USA

Son of the visionary sitarist Shri Rabindranath Lahiri, Shankh Lahiri has devoted his life to Indian classical music. A devoted disciple of world-renowned tabla maestro Pandit Nayan Ghosh, he is an internationally touring tabla artist and founder of the Shruti Foundation and Shruti School of Music in Tampa, Florida. He has performed with Hindol Deb at the Tampa Bay Indian & World Music Festival (2024), the Palladium Theater in St. Petersburg (2025), and the International Guitar Festival in Orlando (2025).

Arup Sen Gupta
Tabla Artist · Germany

Born and raised in Germany, Arup Sen Gupta is one of the most sought-after tabla accompanists in Europe. A disciple of Sri Subhen Chatterjee and Pandit Swapan Chaudhuri (Lucknow Gharana), he has accompanied some of India's finest musicians across Europe, India, and the USA. He performed with Hindol Deb in a memorable livestreamed concert from the LOFT in Cologne in May 2020.

Florian Schiertz
Tabla Artist · Switzerland & Germany

A versatile tabla player based in Switzerland and Germany, Florian Schiertz has collaborated with Hindol Deb for performances in Switzerland, including a concert in Lucerne in 2019 and at the Rietbergmuseum in Zurich in 2021. He has performed with numerous Indian classical musicians across Europe.

Amit Mishra
Tabla Artist · India

An accomplished tabla player from India, Amit Mishra has performed with Hindol Deb in a sitar recital of Raga Gawoti, presented by EKSWAR. His playing is known for its precision, sensitivity, and deep understanding of the Hindustani classical tradition.

The Heritage

A Tradition of Three Thousand Years

The music that Hindol Deb plays in Ragasutra is the living heir to a tradition that stretches back over three thousand years, to the sacred hymns of the Sama Veda. It was nurtured in the courts of the Mughal emperors, who were passionate patrons of music and the arts. It was refined by generations of masters — the dhrupadiya singers, the been players, the sitar virtuosos — each adding their own contribution to the ever-evolving tradition.

The great Mughal emperor Akbar is said to have been so moved by the music of Tansen, his court musician, that he would weep with emotion. The tradition of Indian classical music has always been understood as a spiritual practice as much as an artistic one — a means of approaching the divine through the power of sound.

When Hindol Deb sits on stage with his sitar, he carries this entire tradition within him. Every note he plays is informed by the teachings of his gurus, the wisdom of the ancient texts, and the living spirit of the raga. Ragasutra is his offering to this tradition — a dedication to its purity, its depth, and its timeless beauty.
